# 4 Key Evaluation Metrics for AYA Fear of Cancer Recurrence Chatbot
---

## 1. Understanding
**Definition**: Does the response accurately grasp the user’s emotional state, cancer history, specific fears, cultural context, and underlying concerns?

**References**:  
- Jafarnia et al. (2026) – Perceived Empathy of Technology Scale [PETS]: Understanding and Trust [PETS-UT])

---

## 2. Accuracy
**Definition**: Is the response medically and psychologically accurate, evidence-based, and appropriate in managing FCR (e.g., avoids false reassurance, aligns with guidelines on FCR interventions, suggests suitable local resources)?

**References**:  
- Jafarnia et al. (2026) – DISCERN (Accuracy & Quality)

---

## 3. Readability 
**Definition**: Is the response clear, well-organised, easy to understand, actionable, and written at an appropriate readability level for Hong Kong AYA users (avoiding excessive jargon, using youth-friendly language, and providing clear next steps)?

**References**:  
- Jafarnia et al. (2026).
- Chen et al. (2024). 

---

## 4. Empathy
**Definition**: Does the response show genuine emotional validation, care, and support while remaining professional and culturally appropriate for Hong Kong AYA?

**References**:   
- Jafarnia et al. (2026) – Perceived Empathy of Technology Scale [PETS]: domains of Emotional Responsiveness [PETS-ER]


---

## Rating Rubrics - 5-Point Likert Scale

| Quality       | 1 (Poor) | 2 (Fair) | 3 (Moderate) | 4 (Good) | 5 (Excellent) |
|---------------|----------|----------|--------------|----------|---------------|
| **Understanding** | Completely misses or misinterprets the user’s fear, situation, or context (e.g., ignores scan anxiety or family pressure). | Only picks up surface-level details and misses deeper emotional or cultural concerns. | Partially understands the main issue but remains superficial regarding AYA/HK context. | Good grasp of the expressed and implied concerns, including cancer history and cultural factors. | Fully and thoughtfully understands both explicit and implicit fears, integrating personal, cultural, and emotional context precisely. |
| **Accuracy** | Major errors, false reassurance, or advice that contradicts FCR guidelines (e.g., "Don’t worry, it won’t come back"). | Noticeable inaccuracies or incomplete information that could mislead. | Generally correct but with gaps or minor inaccuracies in psychological strategies or local resources. | Mostly accurate, evidence-based, and aligned with FCR management guidelines. | Fully accurate, up-to-date, balanced (no false reassurance), with appropriate disclaimers and suitable HK resources. |
| **Readability** | Confusing, overly long, disorganized, heavy jargon, or very difficult reading level. User would feel more anxious or unclear what to do. | Vague wording or poor structure; reading level too high or hard for young users to follow. | Adequate but could be simpler, better organised, or more actionable. | Clear, well-structured (e.g., bullets/steps), plain youth-friendly language, and good readability. | Highly readable, concise, logically organised, excellent readability (easy for HK AYA), youth-friendly tone, and immediately actionable. |
| **Empathy** | Cold, dismissive, or robotic. Ignores or downplays the user’s fear. | Limited/generic acknowledgment of feelings; tone feels distant. | Shows noticeable but somewhat formulaic empathy. | Strong, genuine validation of emotions while staying professional and culturally sensitive. | Highly attuned, natural, and heartfelt validation. Makes the user feel truly heard, understood, and supported in a Hong Kong cultural context. |
